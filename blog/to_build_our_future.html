To Build Our Future, We Must Know Our Past: Contextualizing Paradigm Shifts in Natural Language Processing

Monday October 16, 2023

Authors hosted interviews with various NLP researchers in junior, mid, and senior levels. There are many intresting sections, but the most that resonates with me were the 'Exploit-explore cycles of work' and 'Benchmarking culture'. Exploit-explore cycles of work discuss how in NLP there are seminal works that create a lot of intrest in certain areas which results in the entire community flooding into it and attempting to reach for the low hanging fruit. These are great oportunities for junior researchers, but in the longer run are papers that are not very intresting and explore obvious things. After all the fruit has been picked, there are the less obvious works that could perhaps change the way we viewed that seminical approach. I find this intresting because its what I'm currently going through. I see a cool idea and I'm excited by all these ideas that come to my head but more likely than not they have been explored and are not as intresting. It makes me want to take my ideas and fully think them through before I go and pursue anything. But the constant ticking clock in the background keeps telling that I must be churning things out every time despite them being not good quality. 'Benchmarking-culture' is also intresting because recently I've been feeling like NLP has lost its connection to the practical aspect of the world. The papers we write are always attempting to reach the new best score despite this score having zero correlation with how the technology will be used by actual humans. The literature is filled with ChatGPT benchmarks and evaluations that don't really tell us how we should advance the subfield, rather it tells us what are the mistakes are commerical models doing. I think NLP is at an intresting place and currently I dont fully love it, but I hope these hessitations allow me on projects that are more people oriented.
